  [Prompt]
Solve the following problem step by step.

Problem: A store sells notebooks for Rs.45 each. A student buys 3 notebooks and pays with a Rs.200 note. How much change does the student receive? The student then buys 2 pens at Rs.15 each from the change. How much money is left?

Think through this step by step:
Step 1: Calculate the total cost of notebooks.
Step 2: Calculate the change from Rs.200.
Step 3: Calculate the cost of pens.
Step 4: Calculate the remaining money.

Solve:

  [Response]  (4.56s)
**Step 1: Calculate the total cost of notebooks.**
The cost of one notebook is Rs. 45.
The student buys 3 notebooks.
Total cost of notebooks = Cost per notebook × Number of notebooks
Total cost of notebooks = Rs. 45 × 3 = Rs. 135

**Step 2: Calculate the change from Rs.200.**
The student pays with a Rs. 200 note.
The total cost of notebooks is Rs. 135.
Change received = Amount paid - Total cost of notebooks
Change received = Rs. 200 - Rs. 135 = Rs. 65

**Step 3: Calculate the cost of pens.**
The cost of one pen is Rs. 15.
The student buys 2 pens.
Total cost of pens = Cost per pen × Number of pens
Total cost of pens = Rs. 15 × 2 = Rs. 30

**Step 4: Calculate the remaining money.**
The student uses the change received (Rs. 65) to buy the pens.
The cost of the pens is Rs. 30.
Money left = Change received - Total cost of pens
Money left = Rs. 65 - Rs. 30 = Rs. 35

**Answer:** The student receives Rs. 65 in change and has Rs. 35 left.

### When to Use Each Technique in Real-World Applications:

**Zero-Shot Prompting** is ideal for **quick, straightforward tasks** where the LLM is likely to have sufficient pre-training knowledge. Think of basic summarization, simple sentiment analysis, or generating factual statements. It's excellent for initial exploration and prototyping when you need a fast, albeit potentially less precise, result.

**Few-Shot Prompting** is your go-to when you need the LLM to **adhere to specific formats, styles, or understand nuances not covered by its general training**. If you're building a chatbot that needs to respond in a particular persona, or a system that classifies text into custom categories, providing a few well-chosen examples will significantly boost accuracy and consistency.

**Chain of Thought (CoT) Prompting** is crucial for **any task that involves logical deduction or step-by-step problem-solving**. This includes solving math problems, explaining complex scientific concepts, or even debugging code. By forcing the LLM to articulate its reasoning process, you not only get a more accurate final answer but also gain insight into how it arrived there, making it easier to identify and correct errors.

**Tree of Thought (ToT) Prompting** is best suited for **highly challenging, multi-faceted problems that require exploration and strategic decision-making**. This could involve tasks like composing a novel with intricate plotlines, devising complex game strategies, or assisting in scientific research where multiple hypotheses need to be explored. It's about finding the *best* solution among many possibilities.

The **Interview Approach Prompting** is highly effective for **deep dives and intricate information gathering**. Use this when you need to thoroughly understand a complex subject, build a detailed profile of a fictional character, or elicit precise requirements from a client. It allows for a dynamic and iterative refinement of understanding, much like a real conversation.

======================================================================
  KEY TAKEAWAYS
======================================================================

  * Zero-Shot is simplest but may lack precision on nuanced tasks.
  * Few-Shot improves accuracy by providing pattern examples.
  * CoT forces step-by-step reasoning - ideal for math/logic.
  * ToT explores multiple paths - best for open-ended/strategic problems.
  * Interview Approach gathers requirements first - great for design tasks.

  Zero-Shot vs Few-Shot:
    - Few-Shot consistently produces more formatted, accurate answers
      because the examples anchor the model's output style.
    - Zero-Shot is faster to write but relies entirely on training data.
